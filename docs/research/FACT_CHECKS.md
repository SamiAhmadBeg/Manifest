## PARTLY_TRUE

**Claim:** EPOC-X analog front end is band-limited to 0.2–45 Hz (product page 0.16–43 Hz) at 128/256 SPS vs. SENIAM/ISEK sEMG requirement of 10–500 Hz at ≥1000 Hz, meaning surface EMG is physically unrecordable on this hardware and Emotiv's `fac` stream is a low-band artifact classifier rather than an EMG classifier; corollary that no EPOC-X electrode sits on a facial muscle and none over motor cortex (no C3/Cz/C4), which "Ferreira et al., Front. Neuroinform. 2025 (DOI 10.3389/fninf.2025.1625279)" reproduces as chance-level multiclass MI on EPOC-X.

**Correction:** The hardware numbers and the montage/MI findings are correct. Three specifics are wrong, and one of them changes the decision.

CORRECT AS STATED:
1. Bandwidth and rate. Emotiv's EPOC-X product page states verbatim: "0.16 – 43Hz, digital notch filters at 50Hz and 60Hz", "Built in digital 5th order Sinc filter", "2048 internal downsampled to 128 SPS or 256 SPS (user configured)". (The "0.2–45 Hz" figure is legacy EPOC/EPOC+ spec-sheet wording; the brief already flags the discrepancy, so this is not an error.)
2. Montage. The 14 channels are AF3, F7, F3, FC5, T7, P7, O1, O2, P8, T8, FC6, F4, F8, AF4 — all 10-20/10-10 scalp sites, references CMS/DRL at P3/P4. There is no C3, Cz, or C4. Confirmed.
3. The MI result. The paper at that DOI reports 6-class (rest + 5 imagined movements) test accuracy of 0.17–0.36 against a 16.7% chance level, Cohen's kappa "slightly higher than zero in most cases," and attributes the failure to "the absence of central electrodes." Substantively as described.

WRONG — CITATION:
4. It is not "Ferreira et al." DOI 10.3389/fninf.2025.1625279 is Paulina Tarara, Iwona Przybył, Julius Schöning, Artur Gunia, "Motor imagery-based brain-computer interfaces: an exploration of multiclass motor imagery-based control for Emotiv EPOC X," Frontiers in Neuroinformatics, published 12 August 2025. A pitch deck citing a hallucinated first author on its single load-bearing reference is a checkable error; fix it before anyone reads it.

WRONG — THE PHYSICS CLAIM:
5. "Surface EMG is physically unrecordable on this hardware" is false, and the ISEK standard the brief invokes is what refutes it. ISEK's actual text: "The power density function of the surface EMG signals has negligible contributions outside the range 5-10 Hz to 400-450 Hz. The bandwidth of the amplifier-filter should be within this range e.g. high pass 5 Hz, low pass 500 Hz)." The sEMG band's LOWER bound is 5–10 Hz, so the EPOC-X passband (0.16–43 Hz) overlaps roughly 5/10–43 Hz of genuine sEMG. Independently, Whitham et al. 2007, Clin Neurophysiol (PMID 17574912), "Scalp electrical recording during paralysis: quantitative evidence that EEG frequencies above 20 Hz are contaminated by EMG," found 10- to 200-fold power differences above 20 Hz between paralysed and unparalysed states — i.e. most scalp signal above 20 Hz IS muscle, and that lies inside the EPOC-X passband. What is genuinely unavailable is the 43–500 Hz region containing the sEMG spectral peak (~50–150 Hz) and all median/mean-frequency information — so what is impossible is SENIAM/ISEK-COMPLIANT sEMG, not EMG detection.
6. Minor: ISEK's sampling rule is "at least twice the highest frequency cut-off of the bandpass filter," not a flat ≥1000 Hz. ≥1000 Hz follows only from a 500 Hz low-pass. SENIAM's high-pass recommendation is 10–20 Hz; ISEK's example is 5 Hz. "10–500 Hz" is a fair composite but is not a verbatim ISEK figure.

WRONG — THE COROLLARY:
7. "No EPOC-X electrode sits on a facial muscle" is anatomically incorrect. AF3/AF4 sit on the forehead directly over frontalis (a facial muscle, CN VII — the brow-raise generator); F7/F8 and T7/T8 sit over anterior temporalis (muscle of mastication, CN V — the jaw-clench generator). A "10-20 scalp site" and "over a facial/cranial muscle" are not mutually exclusive categories, and the frontal/temporal 10-20 positions are precisely where the contamination literature says muscle signal is maximal — Whitham et al. 2008 (PMID 18329954) found scalp gamma power "maximal adjacent to cranial or cervical musculature."
8. Therefore "`fac` is a low-band artifact classifier rather than an EMG classifier" is a false dichotomy. The dominant in-band content driving clench/brow detection at 20–43 Hz on frontotemporal electrodes is cranial EMG. It is a band-limited EMG classifier. (I could not verify Emotiv's own wording for the mechanism; their glossary and Cortex data-type pages 404'd, and the Cortex data-sample doc describes only the eyeAct/uAct/uPow/lAct/lPow structure with no statement of sensing mechanism.)

WHAT THIS DOES TO THE DECISION: the direction survives — you cannot do standards-compliant sEMG on an EPOC-X, and a hardware change is required for anything resembling a real EMG product. But the stated reason is wrong in a way that inverts the cost calculus. Because band-limited frontotemporal EMG is genuinely present and genuinely discriminative (which is exactly why Emotiv ships clench/brow detection at all), EPOC-X data collection is NOT unrecoverable spend for a 5-command vocabulary (left/right/select/back/home). A small pilot on hardware already owned is defensible and de-risks the fixture spec. The honest pitch line is not "EMG is unrecordable, we're changing instruments" — it is "the 43 Hz corner throws away the EMG spectral peak, so our ceiling on owned hardware is coarse gesture detection; standards-grade sEMG needs a new front end." Also note the MI paper speaks to motor imagery only; using it as evidence about EMG is a category error — it supports the no-C3/Cz/C4 point and nothing about muscle sensing.

UNVERIFIED: the Emotiv EULA §4.5(c)/(k) exposure. Both EULA URLs returned 404 and my web-search budget was exhausted; I make no finding either way, and the brief's claim that the two risks "share one decision" is untested here.

CONFIRMED:

[Insert amount and calculation of the API License Fee below]" — an unfilled drafting bracket in a published agreement.
4. The document is indeed titled "Emotiv Developer License Agreement (deprecated)" and I could locate no successor. A site-scoped search of Emotiv's legal doc directory returns only the EULA, the privacy policy, and this deprecated file. The binding terms genuinely are unlocatable in public.

WRONG — the open-source claim: §6.1 is a chapeau, not the whole section, and reading it alone inverts the meaning. §6.2 carves out open source: "If the Third Party Software will be distributed outside of Your organization as a free or open source software application, You must contact EMOTIV customer service to be added to EMOTIV's free/open source license list." §6.3 then scopes the negotiated DLA to the complement: distribution "but not as a free or open source software application." WRONG IN FRAMING — the threshold: "under 1000 users and less than $250,000 in revenue" is the label for the FREE tier, not a trigger you "cross." The trigger is the adjacent row: "Over 1000 users or $250,000 in revenue." Note that this is disjunctive. This makes the WHY IT MATTERS concern worse, not better: 1,000 users alone triggers the royalty at zero revenue. Two rehab clinics could indeed reach it. One narrowing offset: the royalty is scoped to revenue "directly attributable to the Emotiv product," which is not self-evidently all of top line — but that phrase is undefined on the page and is exactly the kind of ambiguity that gets resolved against you in a negotiation you enter late.

THE BRIEF "DISAGREEMENT" IS NOT A DISAGREEMENT. Both reports are correct about different documents. The 20% figure is on the marketing page and is absent from the license text — I checked directly: the word "royalty" appears exactly once in the Developer License Agreement, in §9.3's unrelated "royalty-free license," and the strings "20," "1000," and "250,000" appear nowhere in it. So "confirmed on emotiv.com" and "unconfirmed in the primary license text" are both true simultaneously. That is not sloppiness across briefs — it is a real and material finding: the commercial term you would model is published only as marketing copy, with no contractual instrument behind it that anyone can read.

BOTTOM LINE for the underlying worry: the core concern survives verification. There is no public, current, operative instrument granting a right to distribute; the fee exhibit is a blank; the royalty is asserted only in marketing. Whether Manifest is exposed today is a separate question this claim does not address — per the repo's own AGENTS.md, the UI shell is keyboard-driven with no Cortex integration in it, so nothing is presently being distributed under these terms. But the "what's your license to ship this?" question remains one with no good documented answer, and the correct action — one email to Emotiv business development asking for the current Developer License Agreement and the actual API License Fee schedule — is unchanged.

## PARTLY_TRUE

**Claim:** Illinois BIPA (740 ILCS 14) covers "a scan of hand or face geometry," has no revenue/headcount/user-count threshold, and carries a private right of action at $1,000 (negligent) / $5,000 (reckless or intentional) per person plus fees — with a $4.7M settlement against Kenvue/Neutrogena's Skin360 app over ~11,000 Illinois users for facial-geometry collection, and 107+ new BIPA class actions filed in 2025. BIPA 15(b) is a collection-and-notice statute, so "processing is local, nothing leaves the browser" is a strong practical defense but a contested legal one.

**Correction:** The statutory core is fully confirmed; four specifics are wrong or materially incomplete.

1) CONFIRMED verbatim. 740 ILCS 14/10: "'Biometric identifier' means a retina or iris scan, fingerprint, voiceprint, or scan of hand or face geometry," excluding "writing samples, written signatures, photographs, human biological samples used for valid scientific testing or screening, demographic data, tattoo descriptions, or physical descriptions." "Private entity" = "any individual, partnership, corporation, limited liability company, association, or other group, however organized," excluding state/local government and Illinois courts — no revenue, headcount, or user threshold anywhere in the Act. 740 ILCS 14/20 grants a private right of action with liquidated damages of $1,000 (negligent) or $5,000 (intentional/reckless), or actual damages if greater, plus "reasonable attorneys' fees and costs, including expert witness fees" and injunctive relief. 15(a) requires a public written retention/destruction policy (purpose satisfied or 3 years after last interaction, whichever first); 15(b) requires prior written notice of collection/storage, disclosure of specific purpose and length of term, and a written release.

2) SETTLEMENT MISATTRIBUTED AND OVERSTATED AS FINAL. The named defendant is Johnson & Johnson Consumer Inc., not Kenvue — Melzer et al. v. Johnson & Johnson Consumer Inc., D.N.J. Kenvue is the corporate successor to J&J Consumer Health, so "Kenvue/Neutrogena" is defensible in substance but wrong as a case caption. More importantly, this received PRELIMINARY approval on Feb 17, 2026 — it is a proposed settlement, not a final judgment. $4.7M, ~11,000 Illinois class members, class period Dec 9, 2019–May 5, 2023, ~$427/member gross and ~$280 net, plus mandated deletion of facial images within 14 days of approval. Critically, Skin360 UPLOADED facial images to company servers. It is not a local-processing case and provides zero support for or against the in-browser defense the claim invokes it near.

3) FILING COUNT IS CONTESTED AND THE TREND IS THE OPPOSITE OF IMPLIED. Sources disagree: National Law Review's 2025 year-in-review says "at least 100 putative class actions"; Legal Newsline / Wirepoints report 150 new BIPA class actions in 2025, DOWN sharply from 427 in 2024. "107+" is within the plausible range but is not a sourced figure, and the framing implies a rising threat when filings roughly a third of the prior year's. Driver: Public Act 103-0769 (SB 2979), signed and effective Aug 2, 2024, which (a) makes repeated collection/disclosure of the same biometric from the same person by the same method a SINGLE violation with "at most, one recovery," gutting the per-scan accrual theory of Cothron v. White Castle, and (b) confirms "written release" includes an electronic signature. The claim omits both. The second point materially lowers the compliance cost — a click-through e-signature consent gate satisfies 15(b); no wet signature needed.

4) THE GAZE/HEAD-POSE QUESTION IS NOT OPEN — the claim missed controlling appellate authority. Zellmer v. Meta Platforms, Inc., 104 F.4th 1117 (9th Cir. June 17, 2024) holds squarely: "For example, scans of face geometry fall within BIPA's list, but are not covered by BIPA if they cannot identify a person," and "face signatures cannot identify and therefore are not biometric identifiers or information as defined by BIPA." The court reasoned the "-er" suffix in "identifier" imposes a necessary identification-capability condition, so appearing on the statutory list is necessary but not sufficient. This is substantially better for Manifest than the claim's "genuinely open" framing. BUT two limits keep it from being a clean win: (i) Zellmer is Ninth Circuit, not binding in Illinois state court or the Seventh Circuit, where a BIPA case against an Illinois-facing product would most likely land; (ii) Zellmer expressly endorses Hazlitt v. Apple, 500 F. Supp. 3d 738 (S.D. Ill. 2020) — decided in Illinois on Illinois law — for the rule that BIPA applies if the data COULD identify, even where the company never uses it to identify. Meta won only because its "face signature" was an unreversible abstract embedding that, per sworn declaration, "do[es] not reveal any geometric information about the detected face... nor do they correspond to facial features like the eyes or nose, or distances between them." MediaPipe Face Mesh is the opposite: 468 3D landmarks that are literally inter-feature geometry and are plausibly identifying. So Zellmer protects a pipeline that retains only derived pose/gaze angles; it does not protect one that computes or retains the landmark mesh. That engineering distinction, not the local-processing question, is the actual dispositive variable — and the claim does not identify it.

5) THE LOCAL-PROCESSING DEFENSE: directionally right, evidence thinner than claimed. 15(b) does bar an entity from "collect, capture, purchase, receive through trade, or otherwise obtain," and Zellmer frames the threshold question as "whether Meta has collected or captured" the identifier. But I found no case squarely holding that in-browser inference with no transmission defeats 15(b), and no case holding the contrary. Contemporaneous virtual try-on litigation is live and going against defendants on other grounds — Svoboda v. Amazon.com, Inc., No. 25-1361 (7th Cir. Dec. 17, 2025) affirmed class certification for Illinois VTO users, and a Gunnar Optiks ruling rejected the healthcare exemption for aesthetic face scans. Calling local processing "a strong practical defense but a contested legal one" is fair as a characterization of uncertainty, but "contested" overstates it: it is largely UNLITIGATED, which is worse, because there is no favorable precedent to point to at the motion-to-dismiss stage.

NET FOR THE THREAT MODEL: the claim's core thesis holds. BIPA has no threshold, has a private right of action, and applies today to a two-person team in a way the neural-data statutes do not. The compliance work is cheaper than the claim suggests (electronic signature suffices; single-recovery cap limits exposure) and the identification-capability defense is stronger than the claim suggests (Zellmer) — but only if the implementation is disciplined about never persisting the landmark mesh. The recommended weekend of work remains prudent; the framing that it is "mandatory or plausibly existential" is somewhat overstated post-PA 103-0769.

## PARTLY_TRUE

**Claim:** Medicare's speech-generating-device policy explicitly excludes "personal computers, tablets or mobile devices that may be programmed to perform the same functions, but do not meet the definition of DME," and separately excludes features "not related to functional speaking," naming gaming software — sourced from ASHA's summary because CMS LCD L33739 and Policy Article A52469 returned HTTP 403 and were not read directly.

**Correction:** The substance of both exclusions is real and current, but the quoted language is not from the primary documents, and the claim omits the one provision that breaks its strategic conclusion.

1. THE 403 IS NOT A REAL BARRIER. Both documents fetch fine with a browser user-agent: `curl -A "Mozilla/5.0 ... Chrome/120.0 Safari/537.36" "https://www.cms.gov/medicare-coverage-database/view/article.aspx?articleid=52469"` returned HTTP 200 (199,026 bytes); same for lcd.aspx?lcdid=33739 and ncd.aspx?ncdid=274. I read all three. The claim's stated evidentiary limitation should be retracted, not carried forward.

2. THE FIRST QUOTE IS NOT IN ANY PRIMARY DOCUMENT. The string "personal computers, tablets or mobile devices that may be programmed to perform the same functions" appears nowhere in L33739, A52469, or NCD 50.1. It is a secondary paraphrase. The actual A52469 text is narrower and more specific: "Desktop, laptop, tablet, smartphone and other hand-held computers (i.e. general computing devices) are not considered DME because they do not meet criteria 3, 4, 6 and 7 above, even though they may serve a medical purpose," and "a device utilizing tablet, smartphone or computer hardware must be designed by the manufacturer to function solely as a speech generation device ... at the time of initial issue." NCD 50.1 adds: "Computers and tablets are generally not considered DME because they are useful in the absence of an illness or injury."

3. THE SECOND EXCLUSION IS VERBATIM AND CORRECT. A52469 and NCD 50.1 both contain: "Specific features of a speech generating device that are not used by the individual who has a severe speech impairment to meet his or her functional speaking needs." Games are named, though in an adjacent bullet rather than the same one: "Any computing hardware or software not necessary to allow for generation of speech, email, text or phone messages.
